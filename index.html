Problem Statement

The Real-World Challenge

Forest fires are among the most devastating natural disasters affecting our planet. Every year, millions of hectares of forest are destroyed across continents — from the Amazon rainforest in South America to the boreal forests of Siberia and the eucalyptus woodlands of Australia. The 2019–2020 Australian bushfire season alone burned approximately 18.6 million hectares, killed an estimated 3 billion animals, and displaced thousands of people. In India, forests in Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, and the Northeast routinely face seasonal fire threats.

The fundamental challenge facing environmental agencies and disaster management teams is not simply detecting a fire — it is predicting where it will spread next. A fire that begins in one corner of a national park can, within hours, engulf vast areas depending on wind speed, terrain, vegetation type, and humidity. Without accurate prediction models, evacuation decisions are made too late, firefighting resources are misallocated, and containment efforts fail.

Why Simulation Matters

Traditional firefighting relies on human observation, satellite imagery reviewed after delays, and experience-based intuition. These approaches are reactive rather than proactive. A computational simulation that models fire spread in advance provides:

  • Predictive power: Agencies can anticipate fire perimeters hours before they occur.
  • Resource optimization: Fire crews and aircraft can be pre-positioned at predicted spread zones.
  • Evacuation planning: Safe corridors and danger zones can be identified and communicated to civilians.
  • Policy decisions: Controlled burns, firebreak installation, and vegetation management can be data-driven.
  • Training: Firefighters can train against simulated scenarios without real-world risk.
Project Objective: This project implements a Breadth-First Search (BFS) based forest fire spread simulator that models fire propagation across a 2D grid of terrain, incorporates environmental factors such as wind, humidity, slope, and vegetation, and generates hourly progression reports to support disaster management decision-making.

Concept Explanation

Why BFS Models Fire Spread Naturally

Breadth-First Search is a graph traversal algorithm that explores all nodes at the current "distance" from a source before moving to nodes farther away. It processes neighbors level by level, expanding outward like ripples from a stone dropped in water.

A forest fire in uniform terrain behaves almost identically. A fire ignites at a point source and spreads to its immediate neighbors (adjacent trees) first. Those neighbors then catch fire and spread to their adjacent trees in the next time step. This outward, layer-by-layer expansion is a perfect real-world analog of BFS queue expansion:

  • Source node = initial ignition point
  • Queue = current fire frontier (burning cells)
  • Visited set = burned cells (already processed)
  • BFS level = one hour of fire spread
  • Adjacency = neighboring forest cells (4-directional or 8-directional)

Illustrative Example — Fire Spreading on a 5×5 Grid

Consider a 5×5 grid of trees (T) with fire starting at center cell (2,2). We trace BFS level by level:

HOUR 0 — Ignition at (2,2) 0 1 2 3 4 0 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] 1 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] 2 [T] [T] [🔥] [T] [T] ← BFS Queue: {(2,2)} 3 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] 4 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] HOUR 1 — BFS Level 1: 4 Neighbors of (2,2) catch fire 0 1 2 3 4 0 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] 1 [T] [T] [🔥] [T] [T] ← (1,2) 2 [T] [🔥] [X] [🔥] [T] ← (2,1) burned; (2,3) burning 3 [T] [T] [🔥] [T] [T] ← (3,2) 4 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] HOUR 2 — BFS Level 2: 8 new neighbors catch fire 0 1 2 3 4 0 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] 1 [T] [🔥] [X] [🔥] [T] ← New frontier expands 2 [🔥] [X] [X] [X] [🔥] 3 [T] [🔥] [X] [🔥] [T] 4 [T] [T] [T] [T] [T] Key: [T]=Tree, [🔥]=Burning (frontier), [X]=Burned out, [W]=Water(barrier)
Key Insight: Each BFS "level" corresponds to one hour. At hour h, every cell reachable from the origin in exactly h BFS steps will be on fire. This makes BFS a natural clock for time-stepped fire simulation. Non-uniform terrain is handled by assigning spread probabilities to each edge, converting the pure BFS into a probabilistic BFS.

2D Matrix / Grid Representation

The terrain is modeled as an N × M matrix where each cell represents a fixed-area patch of land (e.g., 100 m × 100 m in real deployments). Each cell stores:

  • Cell state (integer code)
  • Environmental parameters (stored as parallel arrays or cell objects)

Environmental Factors and Their Effect on Spread Probability

In a uniform BFS, every neighbor has equal probability of catching fire. We extend this with a spread probability function P(src, dst) that computes the likelihood of fire jumping from cell src to cell dst in one time step:

Wind Direction & VelocityAccelerates fire downwind; slows upwindP × W_factor (1.0–2.5 downwind; 0.2–0.5 upwind)0–120 km/h
Vegetation DensityDense vegetation = higher fuel load = faster spreadP × VD (0.1 sparse → 1.0 dense)0.0–1.0
Vegetation MoistureWet vegetation resists ignitionP × (1 − moisture) clamped to [0.05, 1.0]0% dry → 100% saturated
Terrain SlopeFire climbs uphill faster (preheated fuel)P × e^(0.069 × slope°) uphill direction0°–45°

Combined Spread Probability Function

def compute_spread_probability(src_cell, dst_cell, env_params):
    """Compute probability [0,1] that fire spreads from src to dst."""
    base_p = 1.0   # Start at maximum on uniform terrain

    # Wind factor: dot product of wind vector with spread direction
    wind_factor = get_wind_factor(src_cell, dst_cell, env_params.wind)
    base_p *= wind_factor

    # Vegetation density amplifier
    base_p *= dst_cell.vegetation_density

    # Moisture suppressor
    base_p *= (1 - dst_cell.moisture)

    # Slope: fire climbs uphill easily (Rothermel model approximation)
    slope_rad = get_slope(src_cell, dst_cell)
    base_p *= math.exp(0.069 * slope_rad)

    # Temperature and humidity from weather data
    base_p *= (env_params.temperature / 25.0) ** 0.5
    base_p *= (1 - env_params.humidity / 100) ** 1.5

    return min(max(base_p, 0.0), 1.0)  # Clamp to [0, 1]

Algorithm Design

BFS-Based Fire Simulation Algorithm

Our simulation uses a multi-source BFS because a real fire may have multiple ignition points (lightning strikes, human activity, embers carried by wind). The algorithm processes the entire fire frontier simultaneously at each hour, which maps cleanly to the standard BFS queue structure.

Queue Initialization

Before the simulation begins:

  1. Scan the entire N×M grid for all initially burning cells (state = BURNING).
  2. Add each burning cell to a deque with its coordinates (row, col) and hour=0.
  3. Mark all burning cells in a visited set to prevent re-processing.
  4. Store hourly snapshots in a timeline list for reporting.

Neighbor Processing Logic

For each cell dequeued, we examine its 4 cardinal neighbors (N, S, E, W) — or optionally 8 neighbors (including diagonals for oblique wind spread). Each neighbor is processed as follows:

  1. Skip if already visited, burned, water, or firebreak.
  2. Compute spread probability P = compute_spread_probability(src, dst, env).
  3. Sample a uniform random number r ~ Uniform(0,1).
  4. If r < P: mark the neighbor as BURNING, add to queue with hour+1.
  5. After processing all neighbors of a cell, mark it as BURNED (charred).

Hourly Level Separation

Standard BFS uses a single queue and cannot natively separate "levels." We implement hour-level separation using one of two techniques:

TechniqueImplementationAdvantage
Snapshot Separator Store hour timestamp with each queued element; when hour changes, save snapshot Simple, single deque
Double Queue Use current_level and next_level lists; swap after each hour Clean level separation, easy snapshot
NULL Sentinel Insert None marker into queue after each hour's last cell Classic BFS level trick

Our implementation uses the timestamp approach for maximum clarity.

Python Implementation

The following is a complete, fully working Python 3 implementation of the forest fire BFS simulator. It uses collections.deque for the BFS queue, a 2D list for the grid, and displays hourly progression clearly in the terminal. 

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
 FOREST FIRE SPREAD SIMULATION — BFS Implementation
 Course  : Data Structures & Algorithms
 Domain  : Environmental Safety / Disaster Management
 Language: Python 3.x
 Author  : [Student Name]
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
"""

import math
import random
import copy
from collections import deque
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import List, Tuple, Dict

# ── Cell State Constants ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TREE      = 0   # Unburned forest
BURNING   = 1   # Currently on fire
BURNED    = 2   # Charred / burned out
WATER     = 3   # Water body (barrier)
FIREBREAK = 4   # Engineered firebreak (barrier)
OPEN      = 5   # Open ground / bare soil

# ── Display symbols for terminal output ──────────────────────────────────────
SYMBOLS = {
    TREE     : 'T',
    BURNING  : '🔥',
    BURNED   : 'X',
    WATER    : 'W',
    FIREBREAK: 'B',
    OPEN     : '·',
}

# ── Directions: N, S, W, E ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
DIRECTIONS = [(-1, 0), (1, 0), (0, -1), (0, 1)]
DIR_NAMES  = ['N', 'S', 'W', 'E']


# ── Data Classes ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@dataclass
class WindParams:
    speed_kmh: float = 30.0    # Wind speed in km/h
    direction : str   = 'E'    # Cardinal: N/S/E/W
    # Direction vector (dr, dc) mapping
    _dir_map: Dict = field(default_factory=lambda: {
        'N': (-1,  0), 'S': ( 1,  0),
        'E': ( 0,  1), 'W': ( 0, -1),
    })
    def as_vector(self) -> Tuple[float, float]:
        v = self._dir_map[self.direction]
        scale = self.speed_kmh / 60.0
        return (v[0] * scale, v[1] * scale)

@dataclass
class EnvironmentParams:
    wind        : WindParams = field(default_factory=WindParams)
    temperature : float = 35.0   # Celsius
    humidity    : float = 20.0   # Relative humidity %

@dataclass
class CellMeta:
    vegetation_density: float = 0.8  # 0.0=bare to 1.0=dense forest
    moisture          : float = 0.2  # 0.0=dry to 1.0=saturated
    slope_deg         : float = 0.0  # Terrain slope degrees
    burn_hour         : int   = -1   # Hour at which cell caught fire


# ── Spread Probability Calculator ────────────────────────────────────────────
def compute_spread_probability(
        src_r, src_c, dst_r, dst_c,
        meta: CellMeta,
        env: EnvironmentParams) -> float:
    """
    Returns the probability [0.0, 1.0] that fire spreads from
    cell (src_r,src_c) to cell (dst_r,dst_c) in one time step.
    """
    p = 1.0

    # ── Wind factor ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    wv = env.wind.as_vector()                  # Wind direction vector
    spread_dr = dst_r - src_r
    spread_dc = dst_c - src_c
    length = math.sqrt(spread_dr**2 + spread_dc**2) or 1.0
    dot = (spread_dr / length) * wv[0] + (spread_dc / length) * wv[1]
    wind_factor = 1.0 + dot               # Ranges ~0.2 to 3.0
    p *= max(0.15, wind_factor)

    # ── Vegetation density (fuel load) ───────────────────────────
    p *= meta.vegetation_density

    # ── Moisture suppression ─────────────────────────────────────
    p *= max(0.05, 1.0 - meta.moisture)

    # ── Terrain slope (uphill fires spread faster) ───────────────
    slope_boost = math.exp(0.069 * meta.slope_deg)
    p *= slope_boost

    # ── Ambient temperature boost ────────────────────────────────
    p *= math.sqrt(max(0.1, env.temperature / 25.0))

    # ── Humidity suppression ─────────────────────────────────────
    p *= ((1.0 - env.humidity / 100.0) ** 1.5)

    return min(max(p, 0.0), 1.0)


# ── Grid Display Utility ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
def display_grid(grid: List[List[int]], hour: int,
                  stats: Dict = None):
    rows, cols = len(grid), len(grid[0])
    border = '─' * (cols * 3 + 2)
    print(f"\n{'═'*50}")
    print(f"  📍 HOUR {hour:02d} — Fire Spread State")
    print(border)
    for r in range(rows):
        row_str = ' '.join(f"{SYMBOLS[grid[r][c]]:<2}" for c in range(cols))
        print(f"  {row_str}")
    print(border)
    if stats:
        print(f"  Burning: {stats.get('burning',0):3d} cells | "
              f"Burned: {stats.get('burned',0):3d} cells | "
              f"Safe: {stats.get('safe',0):3d} cells")


# ── Statistics Calculator ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
def get_stats(grid):
    stats = {'tree': 0, 'burning': 0, 'burned': 0, 'safe': 0}
    for row in grid:
        for cell in row:
            if   cell == TREE:      stats['tree']    += 1
            elif cell == BURNING:   stats['burning']  += 1
            elif cell == BURNED:    stats['burned']   += 1
            elif cell in {WATER, FIREBREAK}: stats['safe'] += 1
    return stats


# ── MAIN BFS SIMULATION ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
def simulate_forest_fire(
        initial_grid   : List[List[int]],
        cell_metadata  : List[List[CellMeta]],
        env_params     : EnvironmentParams,
        max_hours      : int = 10,
        use_probability: bool = True,
        random_seed    : int = 42
) -> Dict:
    """
    Multi-source BFS forest fire simulation.
    Returns a dictionary containing the final grid and report metrics.
    """
    random.seed(random_seed)
    rows  = len(initial_grid)
    cols  = len(initial_grid[0])
    grid  = copy.deepcopy(initial_grid)      # Work on a copy
    meta  = cell_metadata

    # ── Initialize BFS structures ─────────────────────────────────────────
    queue    = deque()    # Each element: (row, col, hour)
    visited  = set()
    timeline = []         # List of (hour, grid_snapshot, stats)
    hourly_burned = {}    # hour → number of new cells burned

    # ── Seed queue with all initially burning cells ───────────────────────
    seed_count = 0
    for r in range(rows):
        for c in range(cols):
            if grid[r][c] == BURNING:
                queue.append((r, c, 0))
                visited.add((r, c))
                meta[r][c].burn_hour = 0
                seed_count += 1

    if seed_count == 0:
        return {'error': 'No initial fire cells found in grid.'}

    print(f"\n🌲 FOREST FIRE BFS SIMULATION STARTED")
    print(f"   Grid: {rows}×{cols} | Seeds: {seed_count} | Max hours: {max_hours}")
    print(f"   Wind: {env_params.wind.direction} @ {env_params.wind.speed_kmh} km/h | "
          f"Temp: {env_params.temperature}°C | "
          f"Humidity: {env_params.humidity}%")

    # Save initial state
    initial_stats = get_stats(grid)
    timeline.append((0, copy.deepcopy(grid), initial_stats))
    display_grid(grid, 0, initial_stats)

    current_hour = 0
    new_burns_this_hour = 0

    # ── MAIN BFS LOOP ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    while queue:
        r, c, hour = queue.popleft()

        # ── Hour boundary detection ───────────────────────────────────
        if hour > current_hour:
            stats = get_stats(grid)
            hourly_burned[current_hour] = new_burns_this_hour
            timeline.append((current_hour, copy.deepcopy(grid), stats))
            display_grid(grid, current_hour, stats)
            new_burns_this_hour = 0
            current_hour = hour
            if current_hour > max_hours:
                break

        # ── Transition current cell: BURNING → BURNED ─────────────────
        if grid[r][c] == BURNING:
            grid[r][c] = BURNED

        # ── Process all 4 neighbors ───────────────────────────────────
        for dr, dc in DIRECTIONS:
            nr, nc = r + dr, c + dc

            # Boundary check
            if not (0 <= nr < rows and 0 <= nc < cols):
                continue
            # Already processed or barrier
            if (nr, nc) in visited:
                continue
            if grid[nr][nc] not in {TREE, OPEN}:
                continue

            # ── Compute spread probability ────────────────────────────
            if use_probability:
                p = compute_spread_probability(
                    r, c, nr, nc, meta[nr][nc], env_params)
                spreads = (random.random() < p)
            else:
                spreads = (grid[nr][nc] == TREE)  # Deterministic

            if spreads:
                grid[nr][nc] = BURNING
                meta[nr][nc].burn_hour = hour + 1
                visited.add((nr, nc))
                queue.append((nr, nc, hour + 1))
                new_burns_this_hour += 1

    # ── Final state ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    # Finalize any remaining burning cells
    for r in range(rows):
        for c in range(cols):
            if grid[r][c] == BURNING:
                grid[r][c] = BURNED

    final_stats = get_stats(grid)
    display_grid(grid, current_hour, final_stats)

    return {
        'final_grid'   : grid,
        'timeline'     : timeline,
        'final_stats'  : final_stats,
        'hourly_burned': hourly_burned,
        'hours_elapsed': current_hour,
        'cell_metadata': meta,
    }


# ── Area Analysis Report Generator ───────────────────────────────────────────
def generate_report(result: Dict, grid_size_m: float = 100.0):
    """Print a structured area analysis report."""
    stats = result['final_stats']
    total_cells = sum(stats.values())
    cell_area_ha = (grid_size_m * grid_size_m) / 10000   # m² → hectares
    burned_ha    = stats['burned'] * cell_area_ha

    print("\n" + "═" * 55)
    print("  🌲 FOREST FIRE AREA ANALYSIS REPORT")
    print("═" * 55)
    print(f"  Total Cells Simulated  : {total_cells}")
    print(f"  Duration               : {result['hours_elapsed']} hours")
    print(f"  Total Burned Area      : {burned_ha:.2f} hectares")
    print(f"  Cells Burned           : {stats['burned']}")
    print(f"  Cells Remaining (safe) : {stats['tree']}")
    pct = (stats['burned'] / max(1, total_cells)) * 100
    print(f"  Burn Coverage          : {pct:.1f}%")
    print("─" * 55)
    print("  HOURLY PROGRESSION:")
    for h, count in result['hourly_burned'].items():
        bar = '█' * min(count, 40)
        print(f"  Hour {h:02d}: {bar} ({count} new cells)")
    print("═" * 55)

        initial_grid    = initial_grid,

        env_params      = env,

    )

    # ── Generate area report ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    generate_report(result, grid_size_m=100.0)

Time and Space Complexity Analysis

BFS Core Complexity

BFS has a well-established complexity of O(V + E) where V is the number of vertices (cells) and E is the number of edges (adjacency connections between cells). In our N×M grid:

  • Vertices V = N × M — every cell is a node
  • Edges E = 2 × N × M − N − M ≈ 2NM — each interior cell has 4 edges; with a 4-connected grid this gives approximately 2NM edges total
  • Therefore: BFS Total = O(V + E) = O(NM + 2NM) = O(NM)

Scalability Discussion

Challenges at National Park Scale

The Jim Corbett National Park in India spans ~520 km². If we model this at 10m resolution, we get 520 km² ÷ 100 m² = 52 million cells. At 1m resolution (drone-quality): 520 billion cells. At these scales, a naive BFS implementation hits fundamental limits:

  • Memory: 520M cells × 8 bytes = ~4 GB just for the grid
  • Time: Even at 10ns per cell: 52M cells = ~0.5 seconds per BFS pass — manageable, but per-hour simulation with snapshots and metadata multiplies this significantly
  • Snapshot storage: 10 hourly snapshots × 4 GB = 40 GB
  • Real-time requirement: Agencies need updates every minute, not every hour

Optimization Strategies

Spatial Partitioning Divide grid into tiles; only simulate active tiles (ones near fire) O(fire_perimeter²) instead of O(total_area) Border tile coordination overhead
Quadtrees Represent uniform regions (ocean, bare rock, water) as single large cells; expand only where heterogeneous 50–90% memory reduction in parks with large uniform zones Complex implementation for split/merge operations
Sparse Grids Store only non-tree cells (water, firebreaks, burning cells) in a hash map; assume tree everywhere else Memory proportional to active cells, not total grid Hash map overhead vs array access
Multi-Resolution Maps Coarse 100m grid for early prediction; switch to 10m only for active fire zone 10× speedup for overview; precision only where needed Resolution mismatch at zone boundaries
Distributed Processing Partition grid across multiple servers; each handles a region; border exchange via message passing Near-linear speedup with more machines Network latency; partition balancing
Parallel Simulation (CPU) Use Python multiprocessing or C++ OpenMP; process each BFS level in parallel across threads Near O(N×M / num_cores) Shared memory synchronization
GPU Acceleration Map grid to CUDA cores; each GPU thread handles one cell; neighbor updates are embarrassingly parallel 1000× speedup possible (NVIDIA A100 has 6912 cores) GPU memory limits; requires CUDA expertise

Maintaining Responsiveness

For real-time use by emergency management teams, responsiveness is maintained through:

  • Incremental BFS: Run only a fixed number of BFS steps per time slice, yield control back to UI, resume next frame
  • Async processing: Background thread runs simulation; UI thread reads completed snapshots
  • Progressive rendering: Display coarse-resolution updates instantly; refine in background
  • Precomputed risk maps: Cache terrain/vegetation factors; only recompute when weather changes

Future Enhancements

Machine Learning Predictions

A Random Forest or LSTM recurrent neural network can be trained on historical fire data (satellite imagery time series, fire perimeters, weather logs) to predict spread probability far more accurately than our formula. The ML model would replace the compute_spread_probability() function, taking in richer feature vectors and outputting calibrated probabilities. Transfer learning from trained fire models (e.g., FARSITE's ML adaptations) can accelerate deployment.

Satellite Data Integration

NASA's MODIS and VIIRS instruments provide real-time active fire detection at 375m–1km resolution with near-daily global coverage. The Sentinel-2 satellite provides 10m multispectral imagery. Integration pipeline: Download fire hotspot data via NASA FIRMS API → georeference to simulation grid → auto-seed BFS with detected burning cells → update vegetation/moisture layers from NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) and NDMI (Moisture Index) bands.

Drone-Based Monitoring

Autonomous UAVs (drones) can fly fire perimeters and transmit real-time thermal imagery at 1–5m resolution. A drone-to-simulation pipeline would: capture thermal video → detect burning pixels via computer vision → update grid cells in real time → recompute BFS frontier from observed state. This closes the simulation-reality loop, correcting model drift.

Real-Time Weather API Integration

Weather services like OpenWeatherMap, IBM Weather Company, or NOAA provide hourly forecast APIs. The simulation loop can query these APIs at each time step to update EnvironmentParams (wind, temperature, humidity) dynamically. For extreme accuracy, a Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) model (like WRF — Weather Research and Forecasting) can be coupled to provide fire-atmosphere interaction modeling.

GIS Integration

Geographic Information System layers (ESRI ArcGIS / QGIS / GDAL) provide accurate terrain elevation (Digital Elevation Models — DEM), road networks, administrative boundaries, and vegetation classification. Importing GIS data via GDAL/OGR into the simulation replaces synthetic grid data with real topographic detail. Output can also be exported as GeoTIFF or shapefile for use in emergency management systems.

Viva / Exam Questions

1Why is BFS more suitable than DFS for fire spread simulation?
BFS explores all nodes at distance d before nodes at distance d+1, naturally modeling wave-like outward expansion — just like a real fire front. DFS would dive deep into one path (e.g., the northern edge) while neglecting adjacent cells, giving a completely unrealistic fire shape. BFS also naturally produces the shortest-path (earliest ignition time) to any cell, which is critical for evacuation planning. DFS gives no such guarantee.
2What is the time complexity of your BFS-based simulation and why?
The time complexity is O(H × N × M) where H = hours simulated, N = rows, M = columns. The core BFS visits each cell exactly once — O(N×M) — because the visited set prevents re-processing. Each cell has a constant number of neighbors (max 4), and per-neighbor work is O(1) (probability computation + queue insert). Multiplying by H for hourly snapshots gives O(H×N×M). Space complexity is the same due to snapshot storage.
3How do you model hourly time steps in a BFS? BFS doesn't natively have time.
We store a timestamp (hour) with each queue element as a triplet (row, col, hour). When a cell at hour h is dequeued, its neighbors are enqueued with hour h+1. When we encounter a cell with hour = current_hour + 1 for the first time, we detect the level boundary and save a snapshot of the current grid state. This maps perfectly: all cells enqueued with the same hour value are processed in the same "round" — representing one hour of fire spread. Alternatively, we can use a double-buffer approach with current_queue and next_queue.
4What data structure does Python's deque provide and why is it better than a list for BFS?
collections.deque is a doubly-linked list implementation that provides O(1) amortized append() (right end) and popleft() (left end). A Python list's pop(0) is O(n) because all remaining elements must shift left. For BFS with millions of cells, this difference is critical. deque also supports efficient rotation and maxlen-bounded queues for memory management.
5How do water bodies and firebreaks act as barriers in your model?
In the BFS neighbor processing loop, before enqueuing a neighbor, we check its state. If grid[nr][nc] in {WATER, FIREBREAK}, we continue (skip that neighbor entirely). This means fire can never be enqueued from a barrier cell, and thus it can never spread across barriers — exactly modeling their physical containment property. Water is a natural absolute barrier; firebreaks are cleared strips of land that remove fuel, preventing fire crossing.
6Explain the role of the visited set in your simulation.
The visited set prevents a cell from being enqueued multiple times. Without it, a burning cell could be added to the queue by each of its burning neighbors independently, causing exponential redundant processing and incorrect fire spread counting. With the visited set, the first time a cell is marked as igniting, we add it to visited; all subsequent attempts to enqueue it from other burning neighbors are immediately rejected. This maintains the O(N×M) complexity guarantee.
7How does wind direction affect the spread probability in your model?
We compute a wind factor using the dot product of the fire spread direction vector (src→dst normalized) with the wind velocity vector. If fire spreads in the same direction as wind, the dot product is positive, increasing the spread probability (up to 3×). If fire would spread against the wind (upwind), the dot product is negative, reducing probability (down to ~0.15). The magnitude of the wind velocity further scales this effect. This correctly models why fires spread rapidly downwind and slowly upwind in real scenarios.
8What is multi-source BFS and when is it used in fire simulation?
Multi-source BFS initializes the queue with multiple source nodes simultaneously, rather than a single source. In fire simulation, real fires often have multiple simultaneous ignition points (multiple lightning strikes, embers from a main fire starting new blazes). By seeding all initial burning cells into the queue at hour=0, BFS naturally handles them all concurrently — their fronts expand in parallel and may merge. The algorithm itself doesn't need modification; only the initialization changes.
9What is the worst-case space complexity and what causes it?
Worst-case space complexity is O(H × N × M). The dominant factor is the hourly snapshot storage — each snapshot is a deep copy of the N×M grid, and we store H of them. The BFS queue itself is at most O(N×M) in the worst case (when the entire grid perimeter is simultaneously on fire — a ring-shaped fire front). The visited set is also O(N×M). To reduce memory, we can compute statistics on-the-fly instead of storing full snapshots, reducing to O(N×M) total.
10How would you modify the algorithm to find safe evacuation routes?
We run a second BFS from safe exits (park boundaries, roads) BACKWARD through cells that will not be burned within the next k hours. We compare the fire arrival time at each cell (from the fire BFS) with the human evacuation time (from the exit BFS). A cell is on a safe route if evacuation_arrival_time < fire_arrival_time. Cells satisfying this constraint can be connected to form safe corridors. This is essentially a time-dependent shortest path problem layered on top of the fire simulation output.
11Why is BFS classified as a complete and optimal algorithm in this context?
BFS is complete because it is guaranteed to find all reachable cells — it won't miss any cell that fire can potentially reach given unlimited time. BFS is optimal in terms of the minimum number of hops (hours) to reach any cell — it discovers the fastest possible fire path to every cell. This optimality is critical: the fire arrival time at any cell from the BFS represents a lower bound (earliest possible ignition), which is the most dangerous scenario for evacuation planning.
12How would you scale this simulation to handle a 500km² national park at 10m resolution?
At 10m resolution, a 500km² park has 5 billion cells — far too large for a single-machine BFS in memory. I would use: (1) Spatial partitioning — divide into tiles, only simulate active tiles near the fire front; (2) Sparse grid representation — store only non-tree and active cells in a hash map; (3) GPU acceleration — map grid to CUDA cores for parallel per-cell processing; (4) Distributed BFS — partition across multiple servers with border-exchange protocols; (5) Multi-resolution — 100m resolution for overview, 10m only in active zone.
13What is the Rothermel fire spread model and how does it relate to your simulation?
The Rothermel model is a physics-based mathematical model (developed for the USFS in 1972) that calculates fire spread rate based on fuel moisture, wind speed, slope, and fuel bed properties using empirically derived equations. Our simulation uses a simplified approximation of Rothermel's slope and wind factors in the probability computation. A production system would replace our heuristic formula with the full Rothermel equations for the rate of spread (ROS) in m/min, then convert ROS to BFS spread probability at each cell boundary.
14How is the BFS-based simulation different from a cellular automaton (CA) approach?
A Cellular Automaton updates all cells simultaneously at each time step based on local neighborhood rules (like Game of Life). Every cell checks its neighbors and updates state, regardless of whether fire is near. This is O(N×M) per time step even with no active fire. BFS only processes cells on the active fire frontier — significantly more efficient when the fire covers a small fraction of the grid. However, CA supports more complex inter-cell interactions and arbitrary rule sets. Both are valid approaches; BFS is more efficient for the early stages of a localized fire.
15What graph theory concepts underlie your forest fire simulation?
The terrain grid is an implicit graph — vertices are cells, edges connect adjacent cells. The fire simulation is a multi-source BFS on this graph. Barriers (water, firebreaks) represent removed edges. Spread probability converts it to a probabilistic graph traversal. Fire arrival time maps to BFS level / shortest path length. The problem of finding safe evacuation routes is a time-dependent shortest path problem. Fire containment analysis uses graph cut theory — identifying minimum vertex/edge cuts that isolate the fire source from unburned regions.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates how a foundational computer science algorithm — Breadth-First Search — can be directly applied to one of the most pressing environmental challenges of our time: predicting and managing the spread of forest fires.

The natural correspondence between BFS's level-by-level expansion and the outward progression of a fire perimeter makes BFS not just a convenient choice, but a conceptually correct model for this domain. Each BFS level represents one hour of fire spread; the queue is the fire frontier; visited nodes are burned cells; barriers are unreachable nodes. This elegance is the strength of the approach.

By extending pure BFS with environmental factors — wind vectors, vegetation density, moisture levels, terrain slope, temperature, and humidity — we transform a deterministic traversal into a probabilistic physical model. The simulation successfully demonstrates how water bodies and firebreaks contain fire spread, how wind accelerates fire in one direction, and how dry, dense vegetation dramatically increases burn rates.

The O(N × M) time and space complexity of the core BFS makes the algorithm tractable for real deployments at meaningful scales. For truly large national parks, we've identified optimization paths — spatial partitioning, sparse grids, GPU acceleration, and distributed processing — that can extend the approach by several orders of magnitude.

For emergency response teams, the simulation provides exactly what is most valuable: a proactive prediction of where fire will be in the next several hours, along with concrete outputs — burn severity maps, safe evacuation corridors, containment boundaries, and resource recommendations — that directly support life-saving decisions.

Final Takeaway: Breadth-First Search is more than an algorithm — it is a physical model of wavefront propagation. Wherever a phenomenon spreads outward from a source through a network of connected nodes, BFS provides the correct computational framework. Forest fire simulation is one compelling instance of this universal pattern, alongside epidemic spread modeling, network packet routing, and flood simulation. Mastering BFS is mastering the mathematics of spreading processes.
